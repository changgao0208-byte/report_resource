PART A — FOUR CONSISTENCY RULES
         (apply to every section, enforced through writing alone)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

With five people writing in parallel, the report can quietly become
inconsistent in three places: what we predicted, what we found, and
what we call things. 


RULE 1 — A HYPOTHESIS IS ONLY USEFUL IF IT IS COMPARATIVE

Before describing how your method works, say what you expect it to
reveal and why — specifically in relation to the other methods in
the same axis. The expectation does not need to appear in any
particular sentence or position; it just needs to be there, clearly,
before the implementation details arrive.

What makes a hypothesis genuinely useful is the contrast. "We expect
SBERT to capture semantic drift" tells the reader nothing. "We expect
SBERT to capture shifts in meaning that occur even when vocabulary
remains stable — something TF-IDF cannot detect by design" tells them
exactly what distinguishes your method and what evidence would confirm
or undermine it.

A practical check: after writing your Methods paragraph, ask yourself
whether a reader could predict, from your hypothesis alone, what your
result would look like if your method worked and what it would look
like if it did not. If not, the hypothesis is not specific enough.

The seven methods requiring hypotheses, and who is responsible:
  TF-IDF Unigram    — Member 1
  TF-IDF Bigram     — Member 1
  SBERT             — Member 2
  LDA               — Member 2
  Keyword shift     — Member 3
  Cosine distance   — Member 4
  PCA vs. UMAP      — Member 5


RULE 2 — RESULTS MUST ANSWER THE HYPOTHESIS, NOT JUST DESCRIBE A PLOT

When you write your Results paragraph, your reader has already read
your hypothesis. They want to know: were you right? The most common
failure in student reports is a Results section that describes what
the figure shows but never states whether the prediction came true.

You do not need a formulaic confirmation sentence. You do need to make
the connection explicit — whether the method did what you expected,
partially did it, or did something unexpected instead. If your results
surprised you, say so and try to explain why. Unexpected findings are
often the most interesting ones, and the rubric rewards you for
thinking through them rather than glossing over them.

After addressing your own hypothesis, add three short observations —
they can appear anywhere in the paragraph, in any order, in whatever
phrasing fits the surrounding prose:

  One sentence on consistency: does your finding agree with what
  another method found? Name that method and its section reference.

  One sentence on interpretability: can a reader understand what
  drove the change from your output alone, or does it require
  additional analysis? Be honest — SBERT and UMAP are harder to
  interpret than TF-IDF and keyword shift, and that is a legitimate
  limitation worth acknowledging.

  One sentence on alignment with known NLP history: does your
  finding match what we know happened in the field? Cite something.
  The transformer era, the deep learning shift, the move from
  rule-based to statistical methods — these are documented and your
  data should either support or complicate them.

Close your Results section with a sentence that answers the research
question from your method's point of view. Not a summary of the
figure — a direct statement about how NLP research changed, and when.


RULE 3 — AGREE ON SHARED TERMS BEFORE WRITING, THEN USE THEM EXACTLY

Inconsistent naming is invisible to the person writing but immediately
noticeable to a reader who has read all five sections. "Topic model",
"LDA model", and "LDA" all refer to the same thing, but in a report
written by five people they will appear as three different methods
unless everyone consciously aligns.

Use these terms and no others without checking in the group first:

  Axis 1 (text representation)
  Axis 2 (comparison method)

  TF-IDF Unigram  |  TF-IDF Bigram  |  SBERT  |  LDA
  keyword shift analysis  |  cosine distance
  PCA  |  UMAP

  Period names: 1991--1995, 1996--2000, 2001--2005,
                2006--2010, 2011--2015, 2016--2021
  The breakpoint: the 2011--2015 to 2016--2021 transition

  Evaluation criteria (use these exact nouns when evaluating):
    consistency  |  interpretability  |  alignment with known NLP history

When cross-referencing another member's section, use \ref{} to their
subsection label rather than "Member 3's analysis" or similar.
Overleaf automatically generates the correct section number.


RULE 4 — READ THE PRECEDING SECTION BEFORE WRITING YOURS

The Methods section is written in this order:
  Member 1 → Member 2 → Member 3 → Member 4 → Member 5

Before you write, read what came before you, even if it is still a
\TODO block. Three things to check:

  Numbers: are the corpus statistics (N, time range, period bins)
  the same as what you plan to use? If they differ, one of you has
  the wrong figure — resolve it before writing further.

  Bridges: does the preceding section end in a way that leaves your
  section without a logical entry point? If so, open with one
  sentence that connects the two, using \ref{} to link back.

  Overlap: are you about to say something the previous member has
  already said? If so, either cut it from your section or reframe
  it as an extension rather than a repetition.

If the section before yours is not written yet, add a short comment
in Overleaf at any point where you would normally cross-reference,
so you can fill it in once they finish:
  % link to Member N here once written

PART B — UNIVERSAL REQUIREMENTS FOR ALL MEMBERS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The rubric awards marks for evaluating each method on consistency,
interpretability, and alignment with known NLP history (points 4a,
4b, 4c). These evaluations must appear in your Results section, not
only in Member 5's Discussion. The Discussion should synthesise
what you each said, not introduce it for the first time.

You also need to answer the research question from your method's
perspective (rubric point 3). The question is: how has NLP research
changed? Your method should contribute something specific to that
answer — a transition period it identified, a type of change it
revealed, or a limitation it exposed.

In practice this means every Results section should contain, in
natural prose and in whatever order works for your argument:
  — whether your hypothesis was confirmed and why
  — whether your finding agrees with at least one other method
  — how interpretable your output is and what limits it
  — how your finding relates to a documented event in NLP history
  — what your method contributes to the answer to the research question

PART C — MEMBER-SPECIFIC ADJUSTMENTS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

MEMBER 1

  Introduction:
    Your paragraph is the anchor for the whole report. Every other
    member will read it before writing their own section, and the
    marker will read it before reading anything else. The last thing
    it should do is introduce both analytic axes — name them and
    briefly say what each one varies. This does not need to be a
    formal definition; a single clear sentence is enough to orient
    the reader for everything that follows.

  Methods:
    You are writing the baseline, which means your role is partly
    to define the starting point that all other methods depart from.
    Explain why TF-IDF Unigram is the baseline rather than one of
    the improvements — what properties make it a sensible reference?
    Your hypothesis for TF-IDF Bigram should be written in relation
    to TF-IDF Unigram, not in relation to SBERT or LDA, because
    bigram is the closest alternative to the baseline and the most
    natural first comparison.

  Results:
    When you discuss consistency, mention keyword shift analysis
    specifically — your lexical trajectory findings and Member 3's
    keyword findings are measuring related things and the reader
    will want to know whether they agree.


MEMBER 2

  Introduction:
    Match the corpus statistics from Member 1's paragraph exactly.
    If Member 1 has not written yet, add a note to yourself and
    reconcile the numbers before submission.

  Methods:
    Your two methods — SBERT and LDA — represent different ideas
    about what text representation captures. SBERT encodes meaning
    at the sentence level; LDA encodes thematic structure across
    a corpus. Their hypotheses should reflect this difference, not
    simply claim that both are better than TF-IDF. A reader who
    finishes your Methods section should understand not only what
    each method does but what each one uniquely reveals that the
    other cannot. The opening sentence of your subsection should
    connect to Member 1's TF-IDF section using \ref{} — something
    that explains why SBERT and LDA are needed in addition to,
    rather than instead of, the lexical baseline.

  Results:
    Interpretability is a genuine weakness of both SBERT and LDA
    compared to TF-IDF, and you should say so directly. Trajectory
    directions in embedding space do not map onto interpretable
    vocabulary without additional probing; topic labels require
    manual inspection and are not guaranteed to be stable. Naming
    this limitation is a sign of analytical rigour, not a
    concession.


MEMBER 3

  Introduction:
    Make interpretability the first requirement. This sets up your
    method — keyword shift — as directly satisfying a need that
    Axis-1 methods cannot fully meet, which strengthens the
    motivation for Axis 2 as a whole.

  Methods:
    Your hypothesis needs to say what keyword shift reveals that
    the four Axis-1 methods cannot. The contrast should be
    specific: it is not just that keyword shift is more
    interpretable in general, but that it identifies which
    particular vocabulary items are responsible for the shift —
    something that trajectory distance measures cannot do by
    design. Write the hypothesis with enough precision that
    someone could look at your results and say clearly whether
    you were right.

  Results:
    Naming specific terms that rose and fell is more compelling
    than saying "vocabulary changed significantly." The reader
    wants to see the evidence. When you compare your findings with
    Member 4's cosine distance results, be specific about whether
    the transition periods agree — if they do, it strengthens both
    findings; if they do not, the discrepancy is worth explaining.
    If Member 4 has not written yet, leave a comment and return
    to this once they have.


MEMBER 4

  Introduction:
    Your paragraph uses concrete examples to make the abstract
    research question tangible. The examples should do more than
    show that things changed — they should raise the question of
    how systematic and how sudden the change was, because that is
    precisely what your cosine distance method is designed to
    measure. A closing sentence that makes this connection
    explicit will tie your Introduction paragraph to your Methods
    section in a way that feels purposeful rather than incidental.

  Methods:
    Cosine distance and keyword shift are the two Axis-2 methods.
    The reader needs to understand why both are needed. Your
    hypothesis should frame cosine distance as detecting something
    that keyword shift misses — for instance, distributional
    change that occurs even when the most frequent terms are
    stable, or a more precise measure of the magnitude of shift
    rather than just its vocabulary composition. The contrast with
    keyword shift should be the sharpest part of your hypothesis.

  Results:
    Your method is particularly well-placed to answer when the
    change happened, which is one of the two parts of the research
    question (how and when). Make sure your Results section gives
    a clear answer to the "when" question rather than only
    describing the shape of the distance curve. Comparing your
    transition peak with Member 3's keyword findings will be one
    of the more interesting moments in the paper — whether they
    agree or diverge both tell us something meaningful.


MEMBER 5

  Methods:
    Open your subsection with a sentence that explicitly connects
    PCA and UMAP to all four Axis-1 representations — they are
    applied to everything, and the reader should understand this
    scope immediately rather than inferring it. Your hypothesis
    should distinguish between what PCA and UMAP each reveal,
    not simply describe their technical properties. A useful
    framing: PCA shows the global structure of change; UMAP shows
    whether that global story conceals local variation.

  Discussion:
    This section synthesises what all five members found. It is
    the only place in the report where someone looks across all
    the Results sections and draws a single coherent conclusion.
    To satisfy rubric points 4a, 4b, and 4c, structure the
    Discussion around these three questions — you do not need
    subheadings, but each question should have its own paragraph:

    Consistency: across all seven methods, which findings agree
    and which diverge? Where they agree, this is the most robust
    claim in the paper. Where they diverge, the discrepancy is
    itself a finding — what does it tell us about how method
    choice shapes interpretation?

    Interpretability: rank the methods honestly. TF-IDF and
    keyword shift produce findings that trace back to specific
    words. SBERT and UMAP produce findings that require additional
    analysis to explain. This hierarchy should inform the
    recommendations you make at the end of the Discussion.

    Alignment with known NLP history: the breakpoint the data
    identifies should be grounded in what we know happened — the
    release of the Transformer architecture in 2017, the emergence
    of BERT and GPT-style pre-training from 2018 onward. Cite
    prior work that documents this \citep{pramanick_diachronic_2023,
    hall_studying_2008}. If the data does not align with the
    historical record, that is also worth discussing.

    The evaluation table (H/M/L) should reflect what each member
    wrote in their own Results section — read every member's
    interpretability and consistency sentences before assigning
    ratings, so the table does not contradict the prose.

  Conclusions:
    The opening sentence of the Conclusions should be the clearest,
    most direct answer to the research question in the entire paper.
    Everything before it has been building evidence; the Conclusions
    is where you deliver the verdict. Do not open with a restatement
    of the method or a summary of the approach — open with the
    finding itself and let the rest of the section unpack it.
